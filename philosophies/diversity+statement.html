Diversity Statement

August 18th, 2012 was the first day I stepped foot on the US. A fellow Greek senior student, who had been assigned as my mentor, picked me up from the airport and brought me to Davidson, NC where I would begin my first week of international student orientation for freshmen. The tables have seemingly turned since then. As an undergraduate international student, I was eagerly “reaping the benefits” of my mentors’ and teachers’ efforts to help integrate me into the classroom and college community; now, as a graduate chemistry teaching assistant, I strive to create learning environments for undergraduates where not only international students, but all under-represented students, can thrive along with their peers.

Through my involvement in a variety of teaching, learning, teaching-as-research, and professional development experiences, I have become familiar with a variety of circumstances that can affect my students’ learning: some may be working part-time jobs, or even raising a child, which makes it hard for them to utilize time-sensitive resources such as office hours. Some may be suffering from a disability that makes learning in the classroom and laboratory much more difficult. Due to their socioeconomic or cultural background, some of them may face language barriers, stereotype threat, or simply a lower level of preparation compared to the majority of their classmates. On the opposite end of the spectrum, a student’s non-traditional background may offer them unique skills (such as multilingual abilities, code-switching, or a good work ethic) that, as instructors, we should notice and allow students to fruitfully apply to their learning. It is essential that our teaching methods can accommodate the needs of all students, while at the same time, allowing each student to utilize their strengths to succeed in our courses.

As much time as we could spend listing and “studying” how every type of circumstance affects student learning, a lifetime would not suffice for us to be able to perfectly serve the needs of each student. This is why I think that the most important strategy for creating an inclusive teaching environment is the continuous and conscientious assessment of our students’ experience in the classroom, and the subsequent refinement of our teaching techniques – in a few words, listening to our students’ needs. A strategy that I have seen work well is, for example, administering an initial survey at the beginning of the semester that asks students about their goals and expectations for the course, as well as for suggestions on what the instructor(s) can do to support their success in the course. For me, this strategy can be even more effective if the assessment continues throughout the semester, e.g. in the form of post-discussion surveys or by reaching out to students individually – especially if they appear disengaged from or struggling with the course.

As we learn more about our student’s background and needs, the next task is meeting those diverse needs, which can be especially challenging within synchronous learning environments. A number of strategies that I follow have proven helpful for that end: for example, when teaching discussion, I have found that using neutral language, allowing for different paces for completing in-class activities, avoiding assumptions about students’ prior knowledge, and directing students to resources they can consult to “refresh” their knowledge, are all effective techniques for maintaining the engagement of all students. However, I anticipate that, as I am faced with new groups of students, I will need to continuously reflect on, assess, and refine my teaching approach. While I could never know what type of road lies ahead, I am confident that my motivation to improve my inclusive teaching skills will persist; as an underrepresented student myself, I have been fortunate enough to have had teachers who not only accepted, but actively encouraged my contributions to the classroom, so my primary goal as an instructor is to pay it forward and to see as many of my students succeed in my courses and beyond.
